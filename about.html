About the Seventh-day Adventist Church

The Seventh-day Adventist Church is a Christian denomination committed to sharing the gospel of Jesus Christ and preparing people for His soon return.

Our History

The Seventh-day Adventist Church has its roots in the Millerite Movement of the 1840s, which anticipated the soon return of Jesus Christ. After the Great Disappointment of 1844, a small group continued to study the Bible and formed what became the Seventh-day Adventist Church.

Key figures include Ellen G. White, who provided spiritual guidance and helped establish many of the church's institutions.

Global Presence

With over 21 million members worldwide, the Seventh-day Adventist Church operates in more than 200 countries. We are known for our commitment to education, health, and community service.

The church runs a worldwide network of schools, hospitals, and publishing houses, all dedicated to serving humanity and spreading the message of hope.

Our Mission

The mission of the Seventh-day Adventist Church is to proclaim the everlasting gospel of Jesus Christ to all the world, making disciples in all nations and preparing people for His soon return.

"Go therefore and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit, teaching them to observe all that I have commanded you." - Matthew 28:19-20

Our Institutions

Education

Over 8,000 schools and universities worldwide, including Andrews University and Loma Linda University.

Health

More than 200 hospitals and clinics providing compassionate care and promoting wellness.

Publishing

Pacific Press and other publishers distributing literature in multiple languages.

Media

Hope Channel and other media ministries sharing the gospel through television and radio.
